# Discovery D2: PHR research arc + unorthodox recombination framing

Task: discovery-d2-phr (v3 grant assembly). Goal: position pseudo-homologous region (PHR) discovery as an ongoing research program that WorkGraph substrates, not as a 36-month clinical deliverable that the grant promises to deliver.

## PHR research program: established

The foundational result is Guarracino, Buonaiuto, Gomes de Lima, Potapova, Rhie, Koren, Rubinstein, Fischer, Gerton, Phillippy, Colonna, Garrison, et al. (HPRC), "Recombination between heterologous human acrocentric chromosomes," Nature 617, 335–343 (2023), doi:10.1038/s41586-023-05976-y, PMID 37165241 (https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37165241/, https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-023-05976-y). Using an all-to-all alignment of the HPRC draft human pangenome via PGGB, the authors showed that the short arms of acrocentric chromosomes 13, 14, 15, 21, and 22 share extended sequence communities (rDNA, segmental duplications, satellite arrays). Within these shared regions they identified pseudo-homologous regions (PHRs): tracts where heterologous (non-homologous) chromosomes carry sequence so similar that they recombine as if they were homologs, with faster decay of linkage disequilibrium and PRDM9 binding-motif enrichment consistent with active meiotic exchange. PHRs co-localize with the breakpoints of recurrent Robertsonian translocations (a sequence-level confirmation of a 50-year-old cytogenetic hypothesis). The paper closes far more than it opens: it establishes the phenomenon, names PHRs, and shows the analysis is only feasible in graph-aware, reference-free pangenome representations.

## PHR research program: ongoing follow-ups

Several named threads are in active development in Garrison's group and the broader pangenome community.

1. **Unorthodox recombination cataloging.** "Unorthodox recombination," as Erik uses the term, refers to crossover events that violate the canonical model of meiosis (homolog-with-homolog, allele-with-allele). Operationally this includes (a) heterologous recombination between non-homologous chromosomes mediated by PHRs, as in the SAAC result; (b) non-allelic homologous recombination (NAHR) between paralogous segmental duplications elsewhere in the genome (the dominant mechanism behind recurrent genomic-disorder CNVs); and (c) interlocus gene conversion between paralogs without a full crossover. These events are systematically invisible to linear-reference analyses because the relevant haplotypes collapse onto a single reference path. Pangenome graphs make them tractable by representing paralogous and structurally divergent sequence as alternative paths whose recombination signatures (shared k-mer communities, LD decay, PRDM9 motif placement) can be measured directly. The 2023 SAAC paper is one instance of this analysis; the ongoing program is to apply the same methodology genome-wide and across species. See "Increased mutation and gene conversion within human segmental duplications," Nature 617 (2023), doi:10.1038/s41586-023-05895-y, for the parallel paralog-resolution work, and "Genotyping sequence-resolved copy-number variations using pangenomes" (https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.1101/2024.08.11.607269v4.full) for the paralog-specific genotyping infrastructure.

2. **Pangenome-aware rare-disease re-analysis.** Liao et al. and successor cohorts have shown that pangenome graph genotyping recovers SVs (and especially repeat-rich and segmentally-duplicated SVs) missed by standard linear pipelines, including in undiagnosed rare-disease patients. See "Pangenome graphs improve the analysis of structural variants in rare genetic diseases," Nature Communications 15 (2024), doi:10.1038/s41467-024-44980-2 (https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-024-44980-2), and "Pangenome-based identification of cryptic pathogenic variants in undiagnosed rare disease patients," medRxiv 2025.07.08.25330875 (https://www.medrxiv.org/content/10.1101/2025.07.08.25330875v1.full). PHR-aware reanalysis is a natural extension: many of the segmentally duplicated, recombination-active regions implicated in genomic disorders are exactly the regions where PHRs and unorthodox recombination concentrate.

3. **Defining and cataloging variants in pangenome graphs.** A standardized variant catalog over graph-resident PHRs is in active development; see "Defining and cataloging variants in pangenome graphs," bioRxiv 2025.08.04.668502 (https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.1101/2025.08.04.668502v1.full). This is prerequisite infrastructure for clinical PHR re-analysis.

## PHR analysis expansion

The methodology generalizes. Acrocentric-style PHRs are unusual only in their conspicuousness; any species whose genome carries large segmental duplications, satellite arrays, or repeat-rich structural-variant hotspots is a candidate. Active expansion is happening on three fronts: (i) other vertebrate pangenomes built with PGGB (mammalian and bird HPRC analogues), where centromeric and immune-locus regions show PHR-like community structure; (ii) plant pangenomes, where polyploidy and large transposable-element families generate paralog landscapes orders of magnitude larger than the human acrocentrics ("Plant graph-based pangenomics," PMC12237840, https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12237840/, and "Comparative population pangenomes reveal unexpected complexity and fitness effects of structural variants," PMC11844517, https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11844517/); and (iii) clinically relevant complex loci (MHC, amylase, NPIP, KIR), where local pangenome graphs already resolve haplotypes invisible to linear references.

## WorkGraph's role as substrate

PHR research is not a single-team, single-tool problem. A typical PHR investigation involves: build a pangenome graph (PGGB run, days, multiple parameter sweeps), identify candidate PHR communities (odgi, custom analysis), validate against population-genetic signatures (LD decay, PRDM9 motifs, recombination maps), cross-reference clinical or comparative-genomic data, and reconcile with prior catalogs. Each step has its own tooling, its own version, its own parameter set, its own primary author, often its own institution. The coordination problem WorkGraph solves is concrete: every candidate PHR or unorthodox-recombination call carries a persistent provenance chain (which graph build, which haplotype panel, which parameter set, which reviewer accepted it) that survives across sessions, operators, and machines. When a clinical re-analysis attaches to a candidate PHR three years later, the dependency chain back to the original graph build is intact and inspectable, not a chat transcript that ended when someone closed a terminal. That is what "graph-native, persistent, multi-actor coordination" means in this concrete domain: the same DAG that captured discovery captures validation, refutation, and re-use.
